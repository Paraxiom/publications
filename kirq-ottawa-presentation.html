The Gap Kirq Fills

Existing networks are either point-to-point QKD (trusted relays, single vendor) or single-source QRNG APIs (centralized, no verification). Kirq is the first to combine multi-vendor QRNG aggregation + Byzantine fault tolerance + STARK proofs + PQ transport into a single open-source stack. Nobody else does all four.

Build It and They Will Come

The demand side is guaranteed. CSE, DRDC, and federal departments have quantum-safe mandates.
What they lack is local infrastructure to test against.

Government Pull

CNSA 2.0 mandates PQC transition by 2030–2035. DRDC's Quantum 2030 plan explicitly calls for a QKD communications network. CSE needs to evaluate quantum-safe solutions. All of them are in Ottawa. They need a local testbed, not a trip to Sherbrooke.

Academic Pull

University of Ottawa and Carleton have 130+ quantum faculty combined. They're already doing quantum optics and QKD research. What they lack is a persistent, open testbed to validate against. A Kirq node becomes a shared research platform.

Industry Pull

Crypto4A manufactures quantum HSMs in Ottawa. Quantropi does digital QKD from Ottawa. They need a local network to demonstrate their products. A Kirq node is a showroom for Canadian quantum hardware.

Network Effects

Once the node exists, connecting to Quebec's Kirq testbed creates Canada's first interprovincial quantum corridor. QEYSSat launches in late 2026 — an Ottawa ground station link creates a satellite–terrestrial quantum network. Each connection multiplies the value.
